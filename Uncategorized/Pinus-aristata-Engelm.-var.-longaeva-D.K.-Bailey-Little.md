## Introduction
Pinus longaeva (commonly referred to as the Great Basin bristlecone pine, intermountain bristlecone pine, or western bristlecone pine) is a long-living species of bristlecone pine tree found in the higher mountains of California, Nevada, and Utah. In 1987, the bristlecone pine was designated one of Nevada's state trees.
Methuselah is a Great Basin bristlecone pine that is 4,857 years old and has been credited as the oldest known living non-clonal organism on Earth.

## Care Guide
The Great Basin Bristlecone Pine thrives in conditions mimicking its native harsh, high-altitude environment, demanding full sun and extremely well-drained, lean soil. Once established, it requires minimal watering, being highly drought-tolerant, and should never be over-fertilized. Ensure excellent drainage and avoid excessive moisture to prevent root rot.

### Fertilizer
Minimal to no fertilization. Thrives in lean, nutrient-poor soils. If absolutely necessary, a very light, slow-release, balanced fertilizer can be applied once in early spring, but generally not recommended.
